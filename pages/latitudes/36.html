It appears that we may have stumbled upon the answer to the question of where everyone has gone. There's a noticeable influx of people coming to this region, drawn from various parts of the country, particularly from the southern areas. Coming from afar they continue long-standing traditions, like the way people source their food.


In cities like Casablanca, Rabat, and Tangiers, individuals who have migrated from the southern regions often establish small, independent, family-run stores within residential neighborhoods. These establishments, often referred to as 'dukan,' play a vital role in the community. Here, people can conveniently purchase not only food but also other essential items they need for their daily lives. These 'dukan' stores represent a Moroccan tradition that has stood the test of time and continues to thrive in the modern era. In essence, they are a contemporary manifestation of a way of life that has been part of Moroccan culture for centuries.


In addition to the small family-run stores, another integral aspect of Moroccan commerce is the presence of souqs. These souqs can take various forms, ranging from permanent or semi-permanent marketplaces to more makeshift setups, often consisting of a single individual selling vegetables from a cart. While these open-air marketplaces may seem more rudimentary compared to the established family-run stores, they are nonetheless an essential part of Moroccan tradition and commerce.


The souq culture reflects the vibrant and dynamic nature of Moroccan markets. In a permanent or semi-permanent souq, you can find a wide array of goods, from fresh produce and spices to clothing, handicrafts, and household items. These souqs often serve as bustling hubs of local activity and commerce, where people come to socialize, trade, and shop for their daily needs.

The level of prosperity for both sellers and buyers often manifests in the predominant type of stores found in a particular area. The phenomenon of small, family-owned stores that stay open late, typically until 10 or 11 pm, and offer a variety of food and goods is not unique to Morocco. These types of stores have also been a longstanding presence in Paris, primarily run by immigrants from North Africa. This trend dates back to the post-World War II era when France faced a labor shortage, and immigration played a pivotal role in its economic growth. These stores became an essential part of daily life in Paris, often colloquially referred to as l’arabe du coin (which roughly translates to 'the Arab on the corner'). Similar to Morocco, these shops provided a 'modern' shopping experience at a time when there were fewer chain grocery stores. They stayed open late, catering to people who needed to shop after work.


Immigrants from North Africa found these small stores to be a means to maintain a decent middle-class living while also being able to return to their home countries periodically. The operation of such stores proved lucrative, which is why, in the early 2000s, chain grocery stores began opening smaller, market-style outlets that stayed open late to meet the needs of evening shoppers. Their competitive strategy often involved offering lower prices, even selling at a loss, which encouraged consumers to shift away from l’arabe du coin.


In more recent time, advancements in technology, such as self-checkout options, has minimized the need for human interaction during shopping. As a result, Arab immigrants who once owned these small stores transitioned to working as guards at chain-run stores, albeit without the same level of income they enjoyed as store owners. This shift has created a monotonous and often tedious job for them. It raises questions about why economic growth appears to come at the cost of destroying the livelihoods of working-class individuals.
