Marrakech has a rich history, serving as the central urban hub for caravans venturing across the Sahara from Timbuktu. Over countless centuries, it has remained a vibrant center of culture and commerce. In its evolution, Marrakech became a captivating haven for the French at one juncture, followed by a retreat for the Beat Movement. Marrakech's surprising greenery, despite its location on the windward side of the Atlas mountain range, can be attributed to a combination of factors, one of which is its unique access to water resources. While it does receive some humid air from the sea, a significant portion of that humidity is lost before it reaches Marrakech due to the mountainous terrain. So, how does Marrakech maintain its green appearance?

To answer this question, we can turn to the following graph, which illustrates the average flow rate of aquifer boreholes in various regions, using data provided by the British Geological Survey. Notably, the region around Beni Mellal exhibits a range of flow rates, with some areas having a high flow rate while others only have a trickle of water, as indicated by the large standard deviation.


However, what makes Marrakech stand out is its reliance on an aquifer with one of the highest borehole flow rates in all of Morocco, occasionally reaching an impressive 30 liters per second. This is a remarkably high flow rate, considering that the average flow rate for the entire country of Morocco hovers around a mere 4 liters per second. This essentially means that Marrakech is exceptionally water-rich compared to other regions in Morocco, including the densely populated northern areas of the country.


The availability of this substantial underground water source has historically played a pivotal role in Marrakech's ability to sustain lush green landscapes, nourish agriculture, and support its population. It has allowed the city to thrive in an otherwise arid region, making it a vital oasis and a center of culture and trade for centuries.

Since 1975, a clear pattern has emerged from the data. First, there has been a noticeable decline in rainfall levels, indicating a trend towards drier conditions. Simultaneously, the data reveals a consistent rise in average temperatures. The regression line for average temperature appears to follow a steep upward trajectory, suggesting a significant warming trend in the area.


The question arises: Can we use data analysis tools to create a predictive model for weather conditions in 2050, assuming all other external variables remain constant? Using a basic linear regression model, we can make some projections.

This represents only a marginal increase of one degree from the current conditions in 2023. Furthermore, the model suggests a substantial decrease in rainfall, with an estimated 110 millimeters of precipitation in 2050. This signifies an 80 millimeter reduction compared to the current levels.
