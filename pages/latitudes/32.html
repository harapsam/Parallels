As we continue our journey northward, just before reaching our next intended destination, let's embark on a brief detour and pause at a latitude that seems to be causing a touch of confusion - is it 32°24'58", or perhaps 32°29'21"? Regardless of the exact coordinates, one thing remains indisputable: these points of interest are situated on opposite sides of the imposing Al Massira Dam.


During the reign of Hassan II, the government initiated a bold plan, often colloquially referred to as 'a dam a year.' This ambitious endeavor had a straightforward yet crucial objective - to construct a new dam each year. The primary aim behind this endeavor was to establish a stable and dependable water supply system to cater to the needs of Morocco's burgeoning population.


One of the noteworthy outcomes of this initiative was the creation of the Eddahbi Mansour Dam in the picturesque Draa Valley that we have already visited, a testament to the nation's commitment to its water management goals. This dam, along with others, was instrumental in achieving the vision of a 'dam a year.'

On the southern bank of the retention lake, you'll find the quaint town of Ouled Aissa, situated precisely at latitude 32°29'21”. Meanwhile, on the northern shoreline, lies the town of Ouled Essi Masoud, positioned at a latitude of 32°24'58". These two towns, although geographically distinct, face an intriguing administrative conundrum. Despite their relatively close proximity, distinguishing them from one another on paper can be surprisingly challenging.


The curious aspect of this situation arises from the fact that the most direct route between these two towns entails a circumnavigation around the Al Massira Dam, which spans a distance of approximately 12 miles. This circumferential path is necessitated by the physical barrier posed by the lake, making it the only practical route for travel between Ouled Aissa and Ouled Essi Masoud. It's worth noting that this dam, while serving as a vital resource for water retention, also introduces a significant detour for those wishing to travel between the two towns.

Intriguingly, when measured in a straight line, or what is referred to as the Euclidean distance, the gap between Ouled Aissa and Ouled Essi Masoud is only about 6 miles. This seemingly shorter distance highlights the unique topographical challenge presented by the Al Massira Dam, which, in essence, doubles the actual distance one must travel to get from one town to the other. This situation underscores how geographical features can play a pivotal role in shaping the administrative and logistical aspects of a region, often resulting in peculiar quirks like this one, where proximity on the map does not necessarily translate to ease of movement in reality.
