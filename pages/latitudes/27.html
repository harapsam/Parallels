Laayoune

Traveling 400 kilometers northward from Dakhla along the coast, we reach Western Sahara's largest urban hub, Laayoune. Surprisingly, Laayoune differs significantly from Dakhla. Contrary to what we have seen until now, it's not situated on a peninsula. Instead, Laayoune is nestled 16 kilometers inland, alongside the banks of a sheltered desert oasis. Its survival hinges on irrigation from an often precarious water system.


Nevertheless, Laayoune surpasses Dakhla in size and offers more in almost every aspect: a larger population, more sea, more land, and even a greater number of ships, despite the distance of the town from the sea. To underscore Laayoune's regional significance, consider the population distribution in Western Sahara as illustrated in the accompanying graph. Notably, Laayoune, Dakhla and Boujdour are coastal cities, whereas Samara lies 180 kilometers inland. Impressively, approximately three-fourths of the entire population reside in just four urban centers.

Approximately 116 kilometers to the southeast of Laayoune lies the crucial phosphate mine of Bu Craa. The colossal investment of $500 million in Laayoune's new port finds its purpose in facilitating the efficient loading of phosphates onto awaiting ships. It's worth noting that despite Bu Craa's remote location, it lays claim to the world's longest conveyor belt, stretching an astonishing 120 kilometers from the heart of the mine to the coastal depot. This extraordinary infrastructure represents a linchpin of the regional economy, underscoring its pivotal role in sustaining livelihoods.


On a global scale, Morocco has established itself as the second-largest exporter of phosphates, yielding only to China.
