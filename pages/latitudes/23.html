Dakhla

Now that we know where the people have gone, let's journey over there and see what's happening. At 23 degrees 29 minutes north on the coastline, we find ourselves in Dakhla, a well-developed urban center in Morocco situated in the middle of the Western Saharan coastline. Dakhla is a delicate balance between opposing worlds - a convergence of land and sea, a dance between hot and cold. First and foremost, let's examine its geographical features. The following image is sourced from Google Earth, and allows us to pinpoint the town of Dakhla on a map. The town is also situated at the tip of a slender peninsula that extends from the western coast of Africa, just like La Gouira. In total, this peninsula covers an area of 70 square kilometers. It's a rather snug fit indeed.

Although the indigenous inhabitants of Western Sahara, the Sahrawis, have a nomadic tradition, in a postcolonial world, the allure of the sea appears to be quite compelling for them. Given the region's narrow strip of land, the sea assumes a paramount role in sustaining life in the region due to its abundant natural resources. European Union member nations have long exploited these resources in Western Sahara, which has sparked resentment among the Sahrawis. In 1976, after leaving the region, Spain granted Morocco military and administrative control in exchange for fishing rights. More recently, France has facilitated negotiations with Morocco to permit industrial fishing ships from France to operate in these waters. Despite the Sahrawis' appeals to the European Union, which imposed an embargo on Moroccan fishing agreements with other nations in the rich coastal waters, this embargo goes largely unenforced. Royal pressure from Morocco is designed to keep agricultural and fishing trade with Europe flowing.

Separated between two worlds, Dakhla strikes an intriguing balance between land and sea. Dakhla may be a narrow peninsula jutting into the sea, but despite its relatively small surface area, it is densely populated and surrounded by a sea teeming with intense commercial fishing activity. Many of the region's inhabitants have chosen to settle here, akin to other coastal cities. But what draws them here? What opportunities exist, and how can one thrive in this environment? Remarkably, despite its location at the edge of the Sahara Desert, where sand meets the sea, Dakhla thrives in colder temperatures. This pie chart may appear deceptively simple, but it actually involved a two-day process of gathering and analyzing data to create. First, a web scraper collected information about real estate for sale, and this data was pre-processed. Next, a shapefile of the Dakhla Peninsula was created using Google Earth Engine, enabling the measurement of its area. This polygon was then used to clip a GeoTiff file containing polygonss of all of the buildings from HumanData. The areas of all these building polygons were calculated and then combined to find the total are of the built environment. In summary, as of the time of writing, approximately one-fourth of all of the land on the peninsula is available for sale.

When considering prospective land development, exotic vacation resorts might come to mind; however, the land acquisition trend around Dakhla is even more unusual. There is currently a $213 million investment project in agriculture around the city of Dakhla, both on the peninsula and on the mainland. The goal is to increase arable land on the seaward side of the Sahara by 52 square kilometers, which is indeed an amazing feat. How will crops thrive in this extreme environment? Unlike the regional capital of Laayoune to the north, Dakhla receives a fair amount of its water from aquifers. However, with increased agricultural activity, there is concern that the aquifers will be quickly depleted. Irrigation also relies on a wind-powered desalination plant. As part of the regional development plan, Dakhla will have a new port at a cost of $1 billion. Currently, just under 1000 hectares of land near the Dakhla peninsula are used by large-scale agro-industries, primarily owned by French agribusinesses or by 'Les Domaines Agricoles,' a Moroccan agricultural business owned by the royal family. Their annual revenue is estimated at $150 million, and nationwide, they own 12,000 hectares.

You might be wondering what anyone could be growing down here. Drought-resistant crops like olives? Nuts? Argan trees, which have long been used to fight desertification? The answer is blueberries. Morocco is the leading exporter of blueberries to Middle Eastern countries and a significant exporter of blueberries to Europe. The growing season in Dakhla is six weeks longer than in Agadir, a few hundred kilometers to the north, and months longer than the growing season in Europe. Beyond blueberries, Dakhla is also home to cucumber, melon, and tomato farms. Morocco is now the world's third-largest exporter of tomatoes, with annual exports of more than 750,000 tons.
